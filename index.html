Aims and scope

Hands-on with the Higgs Discovery. Understand the experimental effort and challenges on the theory side. Familiarize with techniques that are used to claim a discovery and what it means to measure the Higgs mass or couplings.

Audience: For Freshmen and Juniors. Knowledge of Freshmen Physics, introductory Quantum Mechanics but no Quantum Field Theory or Particle Physics knowledge is expected.

Topics and Schedule

Classes are scheduled for Mondays at 2pm in the CCPP lounge (Meyer building, 5th floor).

  1. February 3: CERN, LHC, ATLAS, CMS, Geneva. Big Science. History: Thompson, Rutherford, fixed target, past colliders, discovery W/Z top tau.

  2. February 10: A theory of nature with symmetry. Fermions, Bosons, Leptons, Quarks and their quantum numbers in the SM. Noether’s theorem, particle masses, charge conservation. Detectors: tracker, EM calo, had calo, muon tracker. Data acquisition and triggers.

  3. February 24: Electroweak symmetry breaking. Mass for Gauge Bosons. Yukawa couplings for Fermion masses. Hadron collider, Protons, Parton Distribution Functions. Higgs production modes and decay channels.

  4. March 3: Monte-Carlo generation, detector simulation. Building a statistical model to test the Higgs hypothesis. Statistics: p-value, upper limit, signal strength. Understand LHC plots. Combination of Higgs searches. Discovery. Couplings. Spin/Parity.

  5. (March 10)
